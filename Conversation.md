Chapter 1: Foundations of Case & The "Oblique" Engine

Why this matters: You cannot form complex sentences without mastering the 'Oblique Case' (changing word endings).

1A. The Masculine Oblique Shift



1A-i. The "-a" to "-e" Transformation: Masculine nouns ending in 'a' change to 'e' when followed by a postposition.

Example: "Larka" (Boy) -> "Larke ko" (To the boy).

Diff: Beginners say "Larka ko" (Incorrect). B2 requires the shift.

1A-ii. The Stable Masculine: Masculine nouns not ending in 'a' do not change in singular oblique.

Example: "Ghar" (House) -> "Ghar mein" (In the house).

Diff: Unlike 1A-i, there is no sound change here, often confusing learners.

1A-iii. The Plural Oblique (The "-on" Rule): All plural nouns in oblique take "-on".

Example: "Ladkon ko" (To the boys); "Gharon mein" (In the houses).

Diff: This applies even if the noun doesn't change in the nominative plural (e.g., Ghar -> Gharon).

1B. Feminine Nuances



1B-i. Singular Stability: Feminine nouns never change in the singular oblique.

Example: "Ladki" (Girl) -> "Ladki ko" (To the girl).

Diff: Unlike Masculine -a nouns, Feminine -i nouns are stable.

1B-ii. The Plural shift: Feminine plurals change ending to "-on" in oblique.

Example: "Ladkiyan" (Girls) -> "Ladkiyon ko".

Diff: Distinction between Subject "Ladkiyan" and Object "Ladkiyon".

1B-iii. Exception Handling: Words like "Mata" (Mother) generally don't shift like "Larka".

Example: "Mata ji ko" (Not Mate ji).

Diff: Honorifics and certain Sanskrit words resist the standard oblique drift.

1C. The Pronoun Oblique



1C-i. The "Ko" Transformation: Pronouns change form entirely with "Ko".

Example: "Main" -> "Mujhe" (not Mujhko); "Vo" -> "Usay" (not Usko).

Diff: "Mujhko" is grammatically okay but sounds childish/poetic; "Mujhe" is the B2 standard.

1C-ii. The "Se/Mein/Par" Base: Using the oblique stem.

Example: "Mujh se" (From me); "Us par" (On him).

Diff: You must know the stem "Mujh/Us/Is" separate from the nominative "Main/Vo/Yeh".

1C-iii. The "K" Possession Block: Possessive pronouns behave like Adjectives.

Example: "Mere ghar mein" (In my house).

Diff: "Mera" changes to "Mere" because "Ghar" is in the oblique case (due to 'mein').

Chapter 2: Pronouns, Reflexives & Honorifics

Beyond "You" and "I".

2A. The Hierarchy of "You"



2A-i. Tu (Intimate/Divine/Insulting): Only for God, very close childhood friends, or abuse.

Example: "Tu kahan hai?"

Diff: Misuse causes major social offense.

2A-ii. Tum (Familiar): Friends, younger family, peers.

Example: "Tum kahan ho?"

Diff: Verb agreement is "ho" (plural logic), unlike "hai".

2A-iii. Aap (Respect/Formal): Elders, strangers, seniors.

Example: "Aap kahan hain?"

Diff: Always takes 3rd Person Plural verb forms ("hain", "aayenge").

2B. The "Apna" Reflexive (Self-Ownership)



2B-i. The Basic Rule: When the subject owns the object.

Example: "Main apni gadi chalata hoon" (I drive my car).

Diff: If you say "Main meri gadi...", it sounds redundant to a native ear.

2B-ii. Third Person Ambiguity: Distinguishing distinct owners.

Example: "Ram ne apni kitab padhi" (Ram read his own book) vs. "Ram ne uski kitab padhi" (Ram read someone else's book).

Diff: In English, "his book" is ambiguous. In Hindi, "Apna" vs "Uska" resolves it.

2B-iii. The "Apne Aap" Idiom: Action done by oneself.

Example: "Darwaza apne aap khul gaya" (The door opened by itself).

Diff: Here "Apne aap" functions as an adverb "automatically".

2C. Demonstrative Pronouns



2C-i. Proximal (Yeh/Ye): This/These/He/She (Close).

Example: "Yeh mera bhai hai."

Diff: Used for physical closeness or immediate topic relevance.

2C-ii. Distal (Voh/Ve): That/Those/He/She (Far).

Example: "Voh mera dost hai."

Diff: Also functions as the default "He/She" regardless of distance if not physically present.

2C-iii. The "Ye/Ve" Plural Clarity: Spoken Hindi often merges singular/plural pronunciation.

Example: "Ye log" (These people) vs "Vo log" (Those people).

Diff: Adding "log" (people) is a common B2 strategy to clarify plurality when pronouns sound similar.

Chapter 3: Adjectives, Comparisons & The "Sa" Particle

Describing the world with nuance.

3A. Agreement Rules



3A-i. Marked Adjectives (-a ending): Change with gender/number/case.

Example: "Achha ladka" -> "Achhe ladke" (Plural/Oblique).

Diff: You must match the adjective to the noun's final state (including oblique).

3A-ii. Unmarked Adjectives: Do not change form.

Example: "Sundar ladka" / "Sundar ladki".

Diff: You cannot say "Sundari ladki". Words like Sundar, Dilchasp, Saaf are immutable.

3A-iii. Mixed Adjective Strings: Handling multiple descriptors.

Example: "Ek lamba, sundar, aur tez ladka."

Diff: Only "lamba" changes (lambe ladke ko); "sundar" and "tez" stay rigid.

3B. Comparisons (The "Se" Method)



3B-i. Comparative (Se Zyada): "More X than Y".

Example: "Chai coffee se behtar hai" (Tea is better than coffee).

Diff: Hindi uses the postposition "from" (Se) to denote comparison.

3B-ii. Superlative (Sabse): "More than all".

Example: "Yeh sabse achha hai" (This is the best of all).

Diff: No separate word for "est" (biggest/best); just "from all" (Sab-se).

3B-iii. Implicit Comparison: Dropping the object.

Example: "Aur bada dikhao" (Show a bigger one).

Diff: "Aur" here means "more", implying comparison without "Se".

3C. The "Sa/Si/Se" Particle



3C-i. Similarity (Like-ish): Resemblance.

Example: "Chand sa chehra" (Moon-like face).

Diff: Adds poetic or descriptive comparison.

3C-ii. Diminutive (A little bit): Softening adjectives.

Example: "Thoda sa paani" (A little bit of water).

Diff: "Thoda paani" is factual; "Thoda sa" is specific/measured quantity.

3C-iii. Vagueness:

Example: "Koi sa bhi" (Any one / Whichever).

Diff: Adds indefinite quality to the selection.

Chapter 4: The "Hona" (To Be) Spectrum

The most complex verb in Hindi.

4A. Identification vs. Existence



4A-i. The "Hai" (Is): Current identity/state.

Example: "Vo bimar hai" (He is sick right now).

Diff: Fact, immediate reality.

4A-ii. The "Hota Hai" (Happens/Occurs): General truth/nature.

Example: "Bachche shaitan hote hain" (Children are [generally] naughty).

Diff: Using "Hote hain" implies a universal rule, not a specific instance.

4A-iii. The "Rehta Hai" (Remains): Continuous state.

Example: "Vo hamesha bimar rehta hai" (He remains/stays sick always).

Diff: Implies persistence over time, unlike simple "hai".

4B. Past Existence



4B-i. Tha/The/Thi (Was): Simple past state.

Example: "Main vahan tha."

Diff: Static past.

4B-ii. Hua (Happened/Became): Event occurrence.

Example: "Kya hua?" (What happened?).

Diff: "Tha" is state; "Hua" is an event/change.

4B-iii. Raha (Stayed/Remained): Continued past state.

Example: "Main chup raha" (I remained silent).

Diff: Implies active effort to maintain the state.

4C. Future & Presumption



4C-i. Hoga (Will be): Future state.

Example: "Kal chutti hogi" (Tomorrow will be a holiday).

4C-ii. Hoga (Must be - Presumptive): Guessing the present.

Example: "Vo ghar par hoga" (He must be at home right now).

Diff: Context dictates if it is Future or Presumption.

4C-iii. Ho (Subjunctive Be): May be.

Example: "Shayad vo naraz ho" (Maybe he is angry).

Diff: Shows uncertainty, uses subjunctive root.

Chapter 5: Present Tense Mastery

Nuances of "Right Now" vs. "Always".

5A. The Habitual Present



5A-i. Routine: "Ta hai"

Example: "Main office jata hoon."

5A-ii. Universal Truth:

Example: "Suraj purab se nikalta hai."

5A-iii. Narrating Past Events (Historical Present):

Example: "Phir vo aata hai aur kehta hai..." (Then he comes and says... [telling a story]).

Diff: Using present tense to make a past story vivid.

5B. The Present Continuous



5B-i. Immediate Action: "Raha hai".

Example: "Main kha raha hoon."

5B-ii. Near Future:

Example: "Hum kal ja rahe hain" (We are going tomorrow).

Diff: Certainty of plan allows continuous tense for future.

5B-iii. Annoyance/Repetition:

Example: "Tum hamesha shikayat kar rahe ho!" (You are always complaining).

Diff: Continuous used to emphasize irritation with a habit.

5C. Stative Verbs (The "Sitting" Trap)



5C-i. Body Posture:

Example: "Main baitha hoon" (I am sitting/seated).

Diff: NOT "Baith raha hoon" (which means the act of lowering yourself).

5C-ii. Wearing Clothes:

Example: "Usne laal shirt pehni hai" (He is wearing a red shirt).

Diff: State of being dressed vs. act of dressing ("Pehen raha hai").

5C-iii. Holding Objects:

Example: "Maine bag pakda hua hai" (I am holding a bag).

Diff: "Pakad raha hoon" would mean "I am in the process of grabbing it".

Chapter 6: Past Tense Deep Dive

Moving beyond "I went".

6A. Simple Past



6A-i. Intransitive (Subject Focus):

Example: "Main gaya."

6A-ii. Transitive (Object Focus + Ne):

Example: "Maine khaya."

Diff: The fundamental split in Hindi Past tense.

6A-iii. The "Tha" Auxiliary Addition:

Example: "Main gaya tha" (I had gone / I went).

Diff: Adding "tha" pushes it further back or emphasizes completion (Past Perfect).

6B. Past Continuous



6B-i. Background Action:

Example: "Jab main aaya, vo so raha tha."

6B-ii. Interrupted Action:

Example: "Main ja raha tha, lekin ruk gaya."

6B-iii. Habitual Continuous:

Example: "Vo aksar gunguna raha hota tha." (He was often humming).

Diff: Rare/Advanced structure combining habit + continuous.

6C. Past Habitual (Used to)



6C-i. "Ta tha" Construction:

Example: "Main cricket khelta tha" (I used to play).

6C-ii. Repeated Instance:

Example: "Hum roz milte the."

6C-iii. Discontinued State:

Example: "Main pehle mota hota tha" (I used to be fat).

Diff: Using "Hota tha" for past states that are no longer true.

Chapter 7: Future Tense & Intent

Will, Want, and Presumption.

7A. Simple Future



7A-i. Gender/Number Agreement:

Example: "Ladka jayega, Ladki jayegi, Hum jayenge."

7A-ii. The "Ga" of Certainty:

Example: "Main zaroor aaoonga."

7A-iii. Future as Command:

Example: "Tum wahin rukoge!" (You will stay there!).

Diff: Using Future tense as a strict order.

7B. Presumptive Future



7B-i. Assessing Present:

Example: "Vo parh raha hoga" (He must be reading).

7B-ii. Assessing Past:

Example: "Usne khana kha liya hoga" (He must have eaten).

Diff: Structure looks Future (Hoga), but meaning is Past Deduction.

7B-iii. Doubt vs Certainty:

Example: "Shayad vo aayega" (Maybe he will come) vs "Vo aata hoga" (He must be coming).

7C. Intentional Future



7C-i. "Wala" Future:

Example: "Main jane wala hoon" (I am about to go).

Diff: Immediate future, closer than "Jaoonga".

7C-ii. Negative Refusal:

Example: "Main nahin jane wala" (I am not going to go / I refuse).

Diff: Emphatic refusal.

7C-iii. "Ja raha hoon" as Future:

Example: "Main bus abhi kar raha hoon" (I'm just doing it now).

Diff: Using continuous for immediate intent.

Chapter 8: The "Ne" Construction (Ergative Case)

The biggest hurdle for learners.

8A. The Core Logic



8A-i. Transitive Only:

Example: "Maine chai pee" (Transitive) vs "Main soya" (Intransitive).

8A-ii. Perfective Tenses Only:

Example: "Maine khaya" (Simple Past) / "Maine khaya hai" (Present Perfect).

Diff: Never used in Present/Future/Continuous tenses.

8A-iii. Object Agreement:

Example: "Ram ne roti khayi" (Ram ate bread).

Diff: Verb matches Roti (F), not Ram (M).

8B. Pronoun Shifts



8B-i. Main -> Maine:

8B-ii. Vo -> Usne:

8B-iii. Kaun (Who) -> Kisne:

Example: "Kisne kiya?" (Who did it?).

Diff: Question word also changes form.

8C. The Exceptions (B2 Level Knowledge)



8C-i. Speaking/Understanding:

Example: "Main bola" (I spoke) OR "Maine bola".

Diff: Bolna allows both, but "Maine kaha" (said) always requires Ne.

8C-ii. The "Lana" (Bring) Exception:

Example: "Main pani laya" (Not Maine).

Diff: "Lana" is functionally transitive but grammatically intransitive (implies "to come with").

8C-iii. Compound Interference:

Example: "Main kha chuka" (I have eaten - no Ne) vs "Maine kha liya" (I ate - Ne required).

Diff: The auxiliary vector determines the Ne. "Chukna" is intransitive; "Lena" is transitive.

Chapter 9: Perfect Tenses (The "Chuka/Liya" World)

Completion and its consequences.

9A. Present Perfect



9A-i. Current Relevance:

Example: "Maine chabi kho di hai" (I have lost the key - and it is still lost).

9A-ii. News/Updates:

Example: "Sarkar ne naya kanoon banaya hai."

9A-iii. "Liya Hai" Preference:

Example: "Maine kar liya hai."

Diff: B2 speakers prefer Compound Verbs (Liya) over simple perfect (Kiya) for completion.

9B. Past Perfect



9B-i. Remote Past:

Example: "Maine use das saal pehle dekha tha."

9B-ii. Sequence of Events:

Example: "Mere aane se pehle vo ja chuka tha."

9B-iii. Counter-Fact:

Example: "Maine socha tha ki..." (I had thought that...).

Diff: Often implies the thought was wrong or changed.

9C. Future Perfect



9C-i. Deadline Completion:

Example: "Kal tak main ye kaam khatam kar chuka hoonga."

9C-ii. Assumption of Completion:

Example: "Ab tak usne chitti parh li hogi."

Diff: Presuming an action is finished elsewhere.

9C-iii. "Chukna" usage:

Example: "Vo ja chuke honge."

Diff: Formal way to say "They must have left."

Chapter 10: The "Compulsion" Matrix

Have to, Must, Should, Supposed to.

10A. External Compulsion (Padna)



10A-i. Present Force:

Example: "Mujhe roz dawai khani padti hai" (I have to take meds daily).

Diff: Routine/Habitual compulsion.

10A-ii. One-time Force:

Example: "Kal mujhe bank jana pada" (I had to go to the bank).

Diff: Past definite event.

10A-iii. Future Inevitability:

Example: "Tumhe manna padega" (You will have to agree).

10B. Moral/Internal Duty (Chahiye)



10B-i. Advice:

Example: "Tumhe aaram karna chahiye" (You should rest).

10B-ii. Past Regret:

Example: "Tumhe batana chahiye tha" (You should have told).

Diff: Adding "tha" turns advice into regret/blame.

10B-iii. Want vs Need:

Example: "Mujhe pani chahiye" (I need/want water).

Diff: Object + Chahiye = Need. Verb + Chahiye = Should.

10C. Logical Necessity (Hoga/Hai)



10C-i. "Na" + Hai Structure:

Example: "Mujhe abhi jana hai" (I have to go now).

Diff: Milder than "Padta hai". Shows plan/intent.

10C-ii. "Na" + Tha Structure:

Example: "Mujhe milna tha" (I was supposed to meet).

10C-iii. "Na" + Hoga:

Example: "Mujhe wahan hona hoga" (I will have to be there).

Chapter 11: Compound Verbs I: Benefaction

Using secondary verbs to add shade.

11A. Lena (Take) - Self Focus



11A-i. Absorption:

Example: "Samajh lo" (Understand it for yourself).

11A-ii. Internal Action:

Example: "Kha lo" (Eat up).

Diff: "Khao" is just eat; "Kha lo" is an inviting suggestion.

11A-iii. Reflexive Benefit:

Example: "Rakh lo" (Keep it).

11B. Dena (Give) - Other Focus



11B-i. Direction Outward:

Example: "Phenk do" (Throw it away).

11B-ii. Assistance:

Example: "Padh do" (Read it out for me).

Diff: "Padh lo" (Read it yourself) vs "Padh do" (Read it to someone).

11B-iii. Finality of Exit:

Example: "Jane do" (Let it go).

11C. Modal Nuance of Vectors



11C-i. Command Softener:

Example: "De do na" (Please give it).

Diff: Compound verbs make imperatives sound less harsh.

11C-ii. Dena vs Lena Conflict:

Example: "Likh lo" (Write it down for your notes) vs "Likh do" (Write it for me).

11C-iii. Tense Restrictions:

Example: You generally don't use these in negative sentences.

Diff: "Main nahin gaya" (Correct); "Main nahin ja gaya" (Incorrect).

Chapter 12: Compound Verbs II: Transition & Force

Suddenness, Violence, and Mistakes.

12A. Jana (Go) - Completion



12A-i. Change of State:

Example: "Vo so gaya" (He fell asleep).

Diff: "Soya" (Slept) vs "So gaya" (Transition into sleep).

12A-ii. Totality:

Example: "Vo sab kuch kha gaya" (He ate everything up).

12A-iii. Loss of Control:

Example: "Main bhool gaya" (I forgot).

12B. Padna (Fall) - Suddenness



12B-i. Accidental Action:

Example: "Vo gir pada" (He fell down suddenly).

12B-ii. Emotional Outburst:

Example: "Vo ro padi" (She burst into tears).

Diff: "Royi" is simple; "Ro padi" is sudden/uncontrollable.

12B-iii. Encounter:

Example: "Dikayi padna" (To become visible/appear).

12C. Dalna, Uthna, Baithna



12C-i. Dalna (Force):

Example: "Maar dala" (Killed violently).

12C-ii. Uthna (Eruption):

Example: "Dard utha" (Pain flared up).

12C-iii. Baithna (Regret):

Example: "Main use kho baitha" (I lost him [and regret it]).

Chapter 13: Modals I: Capability & Success

Can vs. Manage.

13A. Sakna (Can)



13A-i. Physical Ability:

Example: "Main daud sakta hoon."

13A-ii. Permission:

Example: "Kya main ja sakta hoon?"

13A-iii. Possibility:

Example: "Ho sakta hai" (It is possible).

13B. Paana (Manage/Acquire)



13B-i. Success in Action:

Example: "Main nahin aa paya" (I couldn't make it).

Diff: "Saka" is neutral; "Paya" implies distinct effort or hurdle.

13B-ii. Often Negative:

Example: Paana is used 90% of the time in negative sentences for ability.

13B-iii. Opportunity:

Example: "Tum mil paye?" (Did you get the chance to meet?).

13C. Passive Capability (Mujhse...)



13C-i. Inability:

Example: "Mujhse khaya nahin jata" (I simply cannot eat [too full/sick]).

Diff: Suggests physical blocking rather than choice.

13C-ii. Emotional Block:

Example: "Mujhse use dekha nahin gaya" (I couldn't bear to watch him).

13C-iii. Positive Capability (Rare):

Example: "Mujhse yeh ho jata hai" (I manage to do this).

Chapter 14: Modals II: Start, Stop & Allow

Controlling the flow of action.

14A. Lagna (To Begin)



14A-i. The "Ne" Deletion:

Example: "Vo rone laga" (He began to cry).

Diff: Even if transitive, "Lagna" removes the "Ne" requirement.

14A-ii. Infinitive Inflection:

Example: "Barish hone lagi" (Rain started happening).

Diff: Main verb acts like a noun (Hone).

14A-iii. "Lagna" as Feeling:

Example: "Mujhe thand lag rahi hai" (I am feeling cold).

Diff: "Lagna" is the most versatile verb; here it means "to attach/feel".

14B. Dena (To Let)



14B-i. Permission:

Example: "Mujhe sone do" (Let me sleep).

14B-ii. With "Ne":

Example: "Usne mujhe jane nahin diya" (He didn't let me go).

Diff: Dena is transitive, so "Ne" returns in Past tense.

14B-iii. As "Offer":

Example: "Main karne deta hoon" (I allow it to be done).

14C. Chukna (To Finish)



14C-i. Completion Marker:

Example: "Main kha chuka hoon."

14C-ii. "Already":

Example: "Vo ja chuke the" (They had already gone).

14C-iii. Alternative to "Liya":

Diff: "Chuka" is more formal/literary than "Liya".

Chapter 15: The Subjunctive Mood

The world of wishes and maybe.

15A. Formation



15A-i. The Stem:

Example: Main karoon, Tu kare, Vo kare, Hum karein, Aap karein.

Diff: Looks like Future tense without the "Ga".

15A-ii. Third Person Singular/Plural:

Example: "Vo aaye" (Singular) vs "Ve aayein" (Plural).

15A-iii. Irregular Roots:

Example: Lena -> Le; Dena -> De; Hona -> Ho.

15B. Usage - Permission & Suggestions



15B-i. "Shall I?":

Example: "Main chaloon?" (Shall I leave?).

15B-ii. Suggestion:

Example: "Hum bahar khayein?" (Shall we eat out?).

15B-iii. Indirect Command:

Example: "Use kaho ki vo chup rahe" (Tell him to stay quiet).

Diff: You cannot use imperative here; must use subjunctive.

15C. Usage - Uncertainty & Wishes



15C-i. Possibility:

Example: "Shayad vo aaj aaye" (Maybe he comes today).

15C-ii. Wishes:

Example: "Kaash main wahan hota" (I wish I were there).

15C-iii. Purpose Clauses:

Example: "Main mehnat karta hoon taaki safal ho sakoon" (I work hard so that I may succeed).

Chapter 16: Conditionals (The "Agar-To" Logic)

If this, then that.

16A. Future Conditional (Probable)



16A-i. Subjunctive + Future:

Example: "Agar vo aaye, to main jaoonga."

Diff: Using Subjunctive (Aaye) for the condition is elegant B2 style.

16A-ii. Future + Future (Colloquial):

Example: "Agar vo aayega, to main jaoonga."

Diff: Grammatically heavier, but common.

16A-iii. Present + Future:

Example: "Agar barish hoti hai, to hum nahin jayenge."

16B. Counter-Factual (Past Unreal)



16B-i. "Hota/Hota":

Example: "Agar tum aate, to main khush hota" (If you had come, I would have been happy).

Diff: Uses Imperfective Participle for both halves.

16B-ii. Regret:

Example: "Agar maine padha hota..." (If only I had studied...).

16B-iii. Mixed Time:

Example: "Agar usne khaya hota, to ab bhookha na hota" (If he had eaten [past], he wouldn't be hungry [now]).

16C. "Jab tak" (Until/Unless)



16C-i. Time Condition:

Example: "Jab tak tum nahin aaoge, main nahin jaoonga."

Diff: Note the double negative logic (Until you don't come, I won't go).

16C-ii. While:

Example: "Jab tak main yahan hoon, chinta mat karo."

16C-iii. Indefinite:

Example: "Jab bhi" (Whenever).

Chapter 17: Participles (Connecting Actions)

Fluid sentences without "And".

17A. Conjunctive Participle (Kar)



17A-i. Sequence:

Example: "Nahakar aao" (Bathe and come / Come after bathing).

17A-ii. Manner:

Example: "Sochkar bolo" (Speak thoughtfully / after thinking).

17A-iii. Stem Only (Colloquial):

Example: "Dekh ke" instead of "Dekh kar".

17B. Imperfective Adjective (Ta hua)



17B-i. Active State:

Example: "Daudta hua ghoda" (Running horse).

17B-ii. While doing (Adverbial):

Example: "Vo rote hue bola" (He spoke while crying).

17B-iii. Agreement:

Example: "Roti hui ladki" (Crying girl).

17C. Perfective Adjective (Ya hua)



17C-i. Finished State:

Example: "Pata hua phool" (Fallen flower).

17C-ii. Static Posture:

Example: "Baitha hua" (Seated).

17C-iii. Object description:

Example: "Likha hua kagaz" (Written paper).

Chapter 18: Relative-Correlative Clauses (Jo-Vo)

Complex Sentence Architecture.

18A. Person & Thing



18A-i. Jo... Vo:

Example: "Jo mehnat karta hai, vo safal hota hai" (He who works hard, succeeds).

18A-ii. Jise/Jisko (Oblique):

Example: "Jis ladke ko tumne dekha, vo mera bhai hai."

Diff: "Jo" becomes "Jis" because of "Ko".

18A-iii. Jo (Thing):

Example: "Jo kitab mez par hai..." (The book that is on the table...).

18B. Time & Place



18B-i. Jab... Tab (When... Then):

Example: "Jab main pahuncha, tab barish ho rahi thi."

18B-ii. Jahan... Vahan (Where... There):

Example: "Jahan tum ho, vahan main hoon."

18B-iii. Jidhar... Udhar (Whither/Direction):

Example: "Jidhar dekho, udhar pani hai" (Wherever you look, there is water).

18C. Quantity & Manner



18C-i. Jitna... Utna (As much as...):

Example: "Jitna chaho, utna lo."

18C-ii. Jaisa... Vaisa (As... So):

Example: "Jaisa karoge, vaisa bharoge" (As you do, so shall you reap).

18C-iii. Jaise hi... Vaise hi (As soon as...):

Example: "Jaise hi vo aaya, main chala gaya."

Chapter 19: Causatives & Agency

Getting things done.

19A. Direct Causative (First Level)



19A-i. Aana suffix:

Example: "Padhana" (To teach/cause to read).

Diff: Padhna (Read) -> Padhana (Teach).

19A-ii. Semantics:

Example: "Khilana" (Feed) from Khana (Eat).
