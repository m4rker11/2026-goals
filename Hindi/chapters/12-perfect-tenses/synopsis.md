# Chapter 12: Perfect Tenses (The "Chuka/Liya" World)

**Completion and its consequences.**

---

## 12A. Present Perfect

### 12A-i. Current Relevance

The present perfect connects a past action to the present moment.

> **Example:**
> "Maine chabi kho di hai" (I have lost the key - *and it is still lost*)

The action has ongoing relevance to the current situation.

---

### 12A-ii. News/Updates

Used for announcing recent developments or breaking news.

> **Example:**
> "Sarkar ne naya kanoon banaya hai" (The government has made a new law)

---

### 12A-iii. "Liya Hai" Preference

B2 speakers prefer compound verbs for expressing completion.

> **Example:**
> "Maine kar liya hai"

**Key Difference:** B2 speakers prefer Compound Verbs (*Liya*) over simple perfect (*Kiya*) to emphasize completion.

---

## 12B. Past Perfect

### 12B-i. Remote Past

Used for actions that occurred before another past event.

> **Example:**
> "Maine use das saal pehle dekha tha" (I had seen him ten years ago)

---

### 12B-ii. Sequence of Events

Establishes the order of past actions.

> **Example:**
> "Mere aane se pehle vo ja chuka tha" (Before my coming, he had already gone)

---

### 12B-iii. Counter-Fact

Often implies that an expectation or thought was incorrect.

> **Example:**
> "Maine socha tha ki..." (I had thought that...)

**Key Difference:** Often implies the thought was wrong or subsequently changed.

---

## 12C. Future Perfect

### 12C-i. Deadline Completion

Expresses that an action will be completed by a specific time.

> **Example:**
> "Kal tak main ye kaam khatam kar chuka hoonga" (By tomorrow, I will have finished this work)

---

### 12C-ii. Assumption of Completion

Presumes an action has been completed elsewhere.

> **Example:**
> "Ab tak usne chitti parh li hogi" (By now, he must have read the letter)

**Key Difference:** Presuming an action is finished in another place or by another person.

---

### 12C-iii. "Chukna" Usage

A more formal way to express completion.

> **Example:**
> "Vo ja chuke honge" (They must have left)

**Key Difference:** This is a formal/literary way to say "They must have already gone."
